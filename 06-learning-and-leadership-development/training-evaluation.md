# Training Evaluation

I help you answer the question everyone ducks: did the training actually change anything? Not "did they enjoy it" — did behaviour shift and did the business feel it.

## When you'd reach for this
- Leadership wants proof the learning spend is worth it.
- You measure smile sheets and nothing else.
- You need to know whether to scale, fix, or kill a programme.
- Behaviour on the job hasn't moved and you need to find out why.

## What I'll need from you
This is an analysis, so I ask for the data first and never invent figures:
- The programme's original objectives and the baseline from `learning-needs-analysis`.
- Reaction data (Level 1) and any learning/assessment scores (Level 2).
- Behaviour evidence (Level 3): manager observation, on-the-job metrics, 360s.
- Business results (Level 4) and cost data if you want an ROI estimate.
Whatever's missing, I'll tell you exactly what to pull and label every assumption.

## How I'll work through it
1. Confirm what "success" meant against the original objectives.
2. Assess Level 1-2: did they engage, and did they learn it?
3. Assess Level 3: has behaviour changed on the job, and for whom?
4. Assess Level 4: did the business metric the programme targeted move?
5. Where you want ROI, compare the value of the change against the fully loaded cost — and state the causation caveats plainly.
6. Recommend: scale, redesign, reinforce, or stop.

## What you'll get
An evaluation report: findings by Kirkpatrick level, an honest read on behaviour change, an ROI estimate with assumptions, and a clear scale/fix/stop recommendation.

## Watch-outs
- Happy sheets measure catering, not capability. Don't stop at Level 1.
- Behaviour change needs a supportive manager; a flat Level 3 may be an environment problem, not a training one.
- ROI claims invite scrutiny — separate correlation from causation openly.
- No baseline means no evaluation; set it before you deliver, not after.
