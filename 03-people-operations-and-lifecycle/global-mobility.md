# Global Mobility

Moving someone to another country touches immigration, tax, pay, and a family's whole life at once. I help you plan assignments that are compliant, fair, and genuinely liveable for the person moving.

## When you'd reach for this
- Relocating an employee domestically or internationally.
- Setting up a short-term, long-term, or permanent international assignment.
- Untangling visa, work-permit, tax, and social-security questions.
- Drafting an assignment letter and defining the package.

## What I'll need from you
- The person, the origin and destination, and the assignment type and length.
- The role, reporting line, and business reason for the move.
- Family situation and any relocation policy already in place.
- Jurisdiction rules, tax approach, and package norms from `company-context.md`.
This crosses two legal and tax systems — I'll map what applies and flag clearly where you need immigration counsel or a tax adviser rather than guess.

## How I'll work through it
1. Confirm assignment type, duration, and the countries involved.
2. Map immigration: visa or permit route, timelines, and dependencies.
3. Surface the tax and social-security considerations in both locations.
4. Frame the compensation approach — home, host, or balance-sheet — and allowances.
5. Plan relocation logistics and family support.
6. Draft the assignment letter capturing terms, duration, and repatriation.

## What you'll get
An assignment plan: immigration route and timeline, tax and social-security considerations, a compensation framing, relocation checklist, and a draft assignment letter.

## Watch-outs
- Immigration and cross-border tax are specialist and unforgiving — get qualified advice, don't improvise.
- Permanent establishment and hidden tax exposure can be triggered by one assignment; check before you move.
- Assignment failures are expensive and usually family-driven — support the whole move, not just the employee.
- Be explicit about repatriation and end-of-assignment terms up front.
